In part 1, we reviewed the working principles of quantum computers and the problem of quantum error correction (QEC), where researchers are forced to come up with new ways to account for corrupted qubits as classical methods fail.

In classical error correction, the most common error is a bit flip, when 0 becomes 1 and 1 becomes 0. Even though the probability of a bit flip is small, it is high enough to cause serious damage to information systems. To combat this, systems will encode the bit by sending multiple copies of it, usually 3. Specifically, 0 would be sent as 000 and 1 would be sent as 111. In this way, if one bit is corrupted, the other two remain the same and the receiving end can tell that there was an error.

For example, if the sender wants to send 0, they will encode it as 000 and send it off. Letâ€™s say that the middle bit is flipped, so the message becomes 010. However, 2 out of the 3 bits are still 0, so by majority vote, the receiver correctly concludes that the original message was 0. If the probability of an error happening without correction is x (x is a small number, like 1% or 0.01), the probability of an error happening with correction actually shrinks to x2 (for instance, 0.012 = 0.0001, a huge improvement!).

In quantum systems, qubits can be 0, 1, or any state in between (superposition). In addition, qubits are subject to two types of error: bit flips and phase flips.
